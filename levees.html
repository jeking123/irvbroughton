The Levees that Break in the Heart is remarkable chronicle of the people who built the levees that helped to create the Modern South. Specifically, it follows fifteen years in the life of Martha, her adopted son, Michael, and A.H. Hutchens, a prom-inent levee contractor many years her senior. Martha was a successful business owner, who gave up her restaurant and bookstore to join her husband, full-time, for the searing heat and strain of life on the levee. Raised on a plantation in Sun-flower County, Mississippi, the life there was of privilege until the crash of the cot-ton crop when she was forced to move several times and adopt a different life.

Earlier, Martha had longed for a child, and the dream of adoption became a reali-ty one late summer day in Richton, Mississippi, when she drove with her lawyer, Alma, to greet Michael, newly born.

The novel is a love story of an unlikely sort, two brilliant people, born years apart. It is also the story of a friendship as the author, in writing, thinks through his rela-tionship with Michael, a genius-poet, who ultimately commits suicide.  It is the story of Blacks and Whites with a common goal:  preserving the land they love and on which they build their lives. In establishing the narrative’s authority, the author interviewed Dorothy Stanford extensively for the details of life, though the book is clearly a work of fiction.

The cast of characters in the levee camp is fascinating. Theol is a bright African-American mechanic, reputed to be the best mechanic on the river. It was up to him to keep the huge machines going. Fix-it, the man whom they called “Dog-boy,” prepared the tents, the water supply and other urgent details. He dug the pits to hold the giant fires and had an uncanny sense of the earth and how well it would hold against the powerful waters. At some point in the story, he inexplica-bly leaves camp, and Martha and Hutchens must get him back, since he’s so es-sential to their success. Then there’s Esta, a three-hundred pound African-American woman, who cooks opossum, deer and other specialties while main-taining the commissary. Year upon year, she would take a worker as her own, and then they’d part company when the season ended or the crew member has gone off to Arizona to pick cotton or Chicago to work as a janitor.

A goodly percentage of the folks building the levees were African-Americans, and the book details the colorful life as they constructed what is known as “The Amer-ican Pyramids.” The novel also chronicles the racial struggles of the times as well as the triumphs of Blacks and Whites living together and working closely, and suc-cessfully, in the teeming confines of tent camps, working the rivers from Carroll-ton, Illinois to New Orleans.

The story opens in the Old Methodist Hospital in Memphis, where Hutchens is close to death and then flashes back to the levee years, a time of struggle and toil and endless challenge. In a failing state, Hutchens rises from the bed, and calls, “Memphis is under six-feet of water.” In the end, Hutchens, the giant will be missed by many for he has worked the Mississippi, the White and Arkansas Riv-ers—among others—for over fifty years.

In a climactic close, during a late November snowstorm, the levee people watch in desperation as the barge, battered by Mississippi storm winds, attempts to unload the earth-moving equipment—the DW-10s, the DW-20s, the bulldozers, all onto the levee, one struggling machine at a time. The levee is disintegrating and failure will bankrupt them.

     With the death of Michael’s father, Martha, alone, walks the lake wondering the future of her son, whom she calls,“her heart.” What will happen to her fif-teen-year-old precocious and prolific poet remains to be seen?
