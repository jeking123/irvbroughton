A skilled chronicler of living history, lrv Broughton has dedicated the better part of his adult life to capturing a wide spectrum of American life: distinguished writers;  famous producers, old-time Floridians; heroic World War ll fliers whose memories and experience contribute to the rich fabric of America and American life.

 

Writer Fred Chappell once remarked that Broughton's The Writer's Mind is "the best book of interviews I have ever read-- lively in tone, relaxed in manner and sometimes startling in revelation. It is a genuine contribution to American cultural history." Even the most casual reader will sense an intimate, fireside pleasure at Broughton's ability to coax detailed, riveting stories from sometimes otherwise quiet and reserved people. His technique allows his subjects to emerge--and emerge they do: overlooked raconteurs.

 

lrv Broughton earned BA and MA degrees from Florida State University and an MA in Creative Writing from Hollins College (now University)--where he received Academy of American Poets' recognition. He studied under, read or hobnobbed with treasured writers like Kay Boyle, Annie Dillard, Malcolm Cowley, George Garrett and William Jay Smith. His own poetry has appeared in many mainstream literary and academic publications, including The Cimarron Review, The Roanoke Review, The Red Clay Reader, lntro, lconoclast, Alembic, The Hollins Critic and The Florida Historical Review. Broughton's interviews have appeared in magazines like The American Poetry Review, The Blue Hotel and The Western Humanities Review--among others.

 

lrv is credited with discovering poet Frank Stanford when the two first met during a literary conference at Hollins. From that chance encounter grew a powerful friendship and literary relationship, wherein lrv served as his principal publisher throughout the poet's tragically short life. The time they spent together--sometimes on what the pair jokingly referred to as the "Left Bank" of the White River in Arkansas--saw a blossoming of inspired, poetic art and powerful, abiding friendship. ln 1975, Broughton produced a documentary film, lt Wasn't a Dream; lt Was a Flood, which was an award winner that year at the Northwest Film and Video Festival and remains the one seminal documentary record of this prolific artist, who's sometimes referred to as "a modern-day Walt Whitman."

 

Earlier, in 1967, Broughton founded the literary magazine Mill Mountain Review and published early work by numerous writers who have long since earned their remarkable reputations: Margaret Atwood, Wendell Berry and Philip Levine. His magazine publishing later expanded into book publication, and included Stanford's first work in both periodical and book forms. A recently completed play, Still Being Frank, imagines Stanford's return from the grave. lrv is currently at work on a screenplay about the late poet, informed by the close relationship forged over seven years.

 

To date, Irv published sixteen books of poetry, non-fiction and fiction. Always interested in exploring new genres, he has developed a significant body of work in a variety of media, both print and film. A trumpet player in high school, he has stretched his wings and undertaken writing a musical comedy, which he plans to finish in early 2017.

 

Along the way, he focused on capturing for posterity, the stories of our finest writers, an endeavor and labor-of-love begun in 1971. With his own limited resources, he was able to capture Kay Boyle, Kenneth Rexroth, John Crowe Ransom, Miller Williams, William Jay Smith, Malcolm Cowley, Kenneth Burke, Elizabeth Spencer, George Garrett, Richard Eberhart and several other distinguished contributors to the nation's literary heritage. He has completed films on subjects as diverse as Last Call Forever, a film about the closing of a Native America bar and Crazy in the Eye, about the Hmong cultural needlework. A three-time winner of the NW Film and Video Festival, he has shown at various festivals including Ann Arbor Bumbershoot (Seattle) and Spokane International Film Festival.

 

Broughton's colorful travels have led him to meet and converse with many American icons: jazz great Louis Armstrong, Converse All-Stars basketball shoes' Chuck Taylor; Kentucky Fried Chicken's Colonel Sanders. He once spent an afternoon with Pulitzer-Prize winners James Wright and Richard Eberhart, hosted actor John Carradine (Stagecoach) for an afternoon when he visited Florida State on a speaking engagement. Irv  has relished the company of the 132-year-old Charlie  Smith-at  one time  billed as the  "oldest workingman  in America," according to "Ripley's Believe It or Not".

 

His interest in provocative and fascinating personalities, their histories, their lives, their achievements, triumphs and defeats, marks a passion that shows little sign of waning. Since those early days, when Broughton traveled the country in his aging '69 Oldsmobile, he filmed and interviewed many others to form a tapestry of personal stories otherwise likely lost to future generations.

 

Years later, Irv Broughton continues to write, film, poeticize--and help us remember. He recently published a book of anti-drug poems for young people, Gotcha! Watcha! Drugs are a Trap. Active current projects include a novel, The Levees that Break in the Heart, loosely based on Frank Stanford's early family life in the levee culture of the American South, and a further collection of interviews--this time with women fliers--slated to make its appearance in late fall or early January.

 

In his spare time, he enjoys gardening. He also loves to follow his Florida State University football, basketball and baseball teams. (He played basketball there "back in the day.") Every couple of years, he gets a kick out of returning to the FSU basketball team reunions. Recently, at Annual Varsity Day, he stood dwarfed in a photo taken next to a new recruit, one who stands seven-foot-five.
